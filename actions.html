Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria

Film Exposing Japanese atrocities

The “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” film exposes the atrocities that occurred during Japan’s biochemical testing on innocent civilians in China. Through the film, the audience is able to learn how Unit 731’s development was made possible with support from the Japanese Emperor Hirohito and under the command of Shirō Ishii. “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” provides a close analysis and look into how the Japanese government was able to successfully carry out research, including its tactics in choosing the location of the laboratory to be Harbin, a location in China invaded by Japan that was remote, yet accessible enough for this top secret laboratory to develop its research. Additionally, such research was also made possible with the dehumanization of its victims. This evidence of dehumanization is shared in “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” by Fang Zheng-Yu, a Chinese slave laborer at Unit 731 who gives his account of what he witnessed while people were taken on a one way train ride to Unit 731. Fang Zheng-Yu states, “I didn’t know what was happening. Then, I noticed arms sticking out, and heads moving and blood coming out. And then, the Japanese yelled ‘logs’, so I knew those were people” (13:14-13:27). The victims were referred to as logs due to the Japanese treating them as not humans, but pieces of wood that could be cut up and then burned without hesitation. This dehumanization of humans in times of war is what is often described as being the way that these atrocities of war are possible.

The “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” film exhibits a close look at the atrocities victims inside the laboratory had to undergo including bombings, frostbite, exposure to diseases, and live vivisection. Additionally, “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” exposes how victims were sprayed with diseases and plagues in cities and villages outside the laboratory. To support this, the “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” film bases its use of evidence on the research of Professor Sheldon H. Harris who was a historian and professor of history at California State University, Northridge. Sheldon H. Harris was also the author of Factories of Death: Japanese Biological Warfare, 1932-45 and the American Cover-Up which gives the film high credibility as it is based on extensive fieldwork by a historian who has also written a lot of scholarly articles, addressed numerous conferences, and spoke about the Japanese experiments in various countries. Apart from the exhibition of research from Sheldon H. Harris, the “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” film also supports its arguments with the use of primary and secondary sources. There are a wide variety of photographs, notes from the laboratory, videos and interviews shown throughout the film. Some of these first hand accounts included are by people like Yoshio Shinozuka who was a medical researcher at Unit 73, Huan Yuefeng who was a Chinese survivor of Unit 731’s field testing, and Fang Zheng-Yu who was a Chinese slave laborer at Unit 731. Secondhand accounts are also included from people like Li Angu who is a relative of Unit 731 victims and Yoshiaki Yoshimi who is a professor of modern history at Chuo University in Tokyo, Japan. By including a wide variety of accounts and evidence, “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” provides a very well rounded and credible account of what happened in Unit 731. It is important to note that the “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” film was aired on July 7, 2001 on the fourth season of a show called “World Justice” on the History Channel. It is reasonable that “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” was aired on a show involving the theme of justice as the film ends with explicit messages involving visions for justice. “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” stresses how Japan keeps denying the war atrocities and includes what people think the Japanese government should do today including the seeking of justice for victims, the persecution of those who committed such atrocities, the public acknowledgement from the Japanese government of what they have done, and monetary compensation for victims. The “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” also shares how it is not enough to only condemn Japan, but stresses how the United States must also face their involvement in these atrocities and their involvement in not allowing Japan to be prosecuted. It is important to note that the film is produced by Lionsgate which is a Canadian-American company. “Unit 731:Nightmare in Manchuria” is also produced in the English language, so it appears the audience is mainly Americans and people living in the United States. Additionally, the film may be attempting to make people in the United States aware of this event that occurred during World War Two and that their own country had a part in.
